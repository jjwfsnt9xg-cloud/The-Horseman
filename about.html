✦ Natural Horseman ✦

I do not train horses to obey.

I work with them until they no longer need to resist.

I The Moment That Changed Everything

In a fifteen-meter training circle, with a Hanoverian stallion, something happened that forced me to question everything I believed about horses — and about myself.

He rose onto his hind legs and walked toward me. Not far. Not close. Just enough that there was no control left in the situation — only truth.

I had handled kicks, bites, being dragged. None of that had reached me the way this did. This time, I felt the possibility of real damage — not just to the horse, but to myself. The rope was still in my hand. I did not freeze. I stayed.

"

What happened in that moment was not just fear.
It was clarity.

I stepped away for twenty days. When I came back, I was not the same person. For the first time, I could feel everything in the horse's body — the tension before movement, the shift in balance, the rise of fear before it became action.

That was the moment I stopped trying to make horses do things.
II Working Without Escalation
Most training systems
  • Resistance→Increase pressure
  • Hesitation→Push forward
  • Reaction→Control harder

This can produce results. But it often creates suppression, not understanding.

My approach is structured around three principles:

I
Remove unnecessary pressure

so the horse can think

II
Allow expression without escalation

so behavior reveals its cause

III
Reinforce willingness

so correct responses are offered, not forced

PRACTICE
In Practice

One warmblood would consistently rush and lock into a single corner of the arena. Traditional correction increased tension and made the pattern stronger.

I did not interrupt the pattern. I allowed the horse to move, reduced pressure, and introduced consistency in my presence. Over time, the horse began to regulate itself — movement improved, tension reduced, willingness appeared without force.

When conflict is reduced and clarity is consistent, the horse begins to offer the correct behavior on its own.

What Horses Taught Me

At one point, I believed I was fearless. Standing at 54 kilograms beside a thousand-pound animal changed that completely.

I had to face fear, shame, self-doubt — the possibility that I was not strong enough for this work.

Strength is not physical.
It is the ability to remain clear and stable
when the situation is not.

Horses do not respond to force.
They respond to that stability.

I do not work with control-driven handling. A horse cannot find clarity through a human who is internally conflicted. Emotional control in the handler is not optional — it is fundamental.
