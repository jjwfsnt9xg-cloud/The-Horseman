I do not train horses

I work with them until they no longer feel the need to resist

Scroll

The Work

For over eleven years, I have worked with horses in a way that does not rely on force, domination, or mechanical repetition. I did not begin with a philosophy. I began like most people do, trying to make things work, trying to control outcomes, trying to get the horse to respond correctly.

Over time, that approach stopped making sense. What I do now is not about making a horse perform. It is about understanding what the horse is experiencing in each moment, what it feels, what it anticipates, and why it reacts the way it does.

Every behaviour, whether calm or explosive, comes from a place that can be observed and understood. My work is built around that understanding.

The Shift

There was a point where the usual methods stopped working, not because they failed to produce results, but because they failed to produce clarity.

I began to notice that a horse could comply and still remain tense, disconnected, or uncertain. The behaviour would look correct, but something underneath it remained unresolved.

Instead of asking, "How do I get the horse to do this?", the question became: Why does the horse feel the need to resist in the first place?

Everything I do now comes from that shift.

How I Work

Most approaches to training respond to resistance by increasing pressure. Sometimes this works. But often, it only suppresses the visible behaviour while leaving the cause untouched.

When a horse resists, I do not immediately correct it. I observe it. I reduce unnecessary pressure. I allow space for the horse to process instead of react. I focus on removing confusion rather than overpowering it.

This does not always look impressive in the moment. It is slower in the beginning. But it produces something far more stable.

A horse that understands, rather than a horse that complies

Core Principles

A horse must be able to think before it can learn.
If the horse is overwhelmed, no amount of pressure will create clarity.

Behaviour is information, not disobedience.
Every reaction shows you something about the horse's internal state.

Willingness is more valuable than obedience.
A correct response given freely is fundamentally different from one that is forced.

Experience

Over the past eleven years, I have worked with a range of horses across different temperaments and conditions. Stallions, highly reactive horses, and individuals with deeply ingrained behavioural patterns.

Much of my work has involved fear based reactions, rearing, escalation, and horses that have been misunderstood or pushed too far.

These are not unusual cases. They are simply situations where the underlying issue has not been addressed.

What I Offer

I do not offer shortcuts, and I do not promise instant results.

What I offer is a way of working that prioritizes clarity over control, stability over speed, and understanding over repetition.

If someone comes to me, they should expect a process, not a quick solution.

What Horses Taught Me

There was a time I believed I was confident, even fearless. That belief does not survive long when you stand next to a thousand kilogram animal that can read every inconsistency in you.

Horses do not respond to what you say. They respond to what you are.

They forced me to confront fear, hesitation, and lack of clarity in a way nothing else could. Over time, the work became less about technique and more about presence.

Boundary

I do not work with dominance based or suppression based handling.

A horse cannot find clarity through a handler who is internally conflicted or externally aggressive. Emotional steadiness is not optional in this work. It is the foundation of it.

A horse does not need to be made obedient. It needs to feel safe enough to respond.

That is where my work begins.
