✦ Natural Horsemanship ✦

I do not train horses
to obey.

I work with them until they no longer feel the need to resist.

I The Moment That Changed Everything

It happened inside a fifteen-metre circle with a Hanoverian stallion. I had handled difficult horses before — kicks, bolts, near-misses. But nothing had reached me the way this did.

He rose onto his hind legs and walked toward me. Slowly. Not in a panic, not in aggression — deliberately. In that moment I had no control over the situation. There was nothing left but truth between us.

"

It was not just fear I felt.
It was clarity — the clearest I had ever been.

I stepped away from horses for twenty days. When I came back, I was different. For the first time I could feel tension before it became movement, and fear before it became reaction. That gap — between stimulus and response — is where everything I now do begins.

That was the moment I stopped trying to make horses do things, and started learning how to make it safe for them to choose.

Horses are honest.
They only resist what does not make sense to them.

II Working Without Escalation

Most conventional training responds to resistance with increased pressure. It can produce results — but it often produces suppression rather than understanding. The horse complies, but the root cause of the behaviour stays buried.

Conventional approach
  • Resistance → Increase pressure
  • Hesitation → Push through it
  • Reaction → Apply more control

This can work. It rarely teaches.

My approach
  • Resistance → Reduce pressure, observe
  • Hesitation → Create space to think
  • Reaction → Find the cause, not the symptom

Slower at first. Far more stable long-term.

Everything I do rests on three principles:

I
Remove unnecessary pressure

A horse that is overwhelmed cannot think. Before you can teach, you have to create calm.

II
Allow expression without escalation

When a horse acts out, that behaviour is information. Shutting it down hides what you need to see.

III
Reinforce willingness, not compliance

A horse that offers the correct response on its own is fundamentally different from one that was forced into it.

In Practice

One warmblood mare I worked with would lock into a corner of the arena and rush every time she approached it. Traditional correction made her worse.

I stopped correcting her. Instead I reduced every form of pressure I was adding — position, energy, expectation. I simply became consistent and quiet in her presence. Within three weeks she began to regulate herself without any intervention from me.

When confusion is removed and the handler is stable, the horse begins to offer the right answer on its own.

What Horses Taught Me

I spent years believing I was fearless. Standing at 54 kilograms beside a thousand-kilogram animal changed that — permanently.

This work forced me to face fear, self-doubt, and the honest question of whether I was actually capable. Horses do not allow you to pretend.

Real strength is not physical.

It is the ability to remain clear and grounded
when everything around you is not.

Horses do not respond to force.
They respond to that stability.

I do not work with dominance-based or suppression-based handling. A horse cannot find clarity through a handler who is internally conflicted. Emotional steadiness is not an optional quality in this work — it is the foundation of it.
