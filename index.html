The Author Behind the Journal

I Did Not Choose Horses —
They Chose Me

Eleven years of silence, surrender, and presence.

This journal was not planned. It emerged from eleven years spent in the company of horses. Not as a trainer who demands obedience, but as a student who learned to listen. The work is not about making a horse perform. It is about understanding what the horse is experiencing in each moment — what it feels, what it anticipates, and why it reacts the way it does.

Every behaviour, whether calm or explosive, comes from a place that can be observed and understood. My work is built around that understanding. Over time, the usual methods stopped working — not because they failed to produce results, but because they failed to produce clarity.

A horse does not react to your hands first. It reacts to your inner state, to what is beneath the hands, beneath the voice, beneath the careful posture. What I had been giving for years was technique wrapped around tension. What I began to give instead was silence. Real silence. The kind that is not absence but presence without demand.

Horses taught me that strength was never the point. What a horse asks for has nothing to do with dominance or performance. It asks you to stand without fear, to be consistent without agenda, to become the kind of presence an intelligent creature of pure instinct chooses to trust. That is a much harder thing than it sounds.

"The horse does not care what you know. It cares who you are in this moment."

Common Questions

Straight from the arena. No theories. Only what works.

Why does my horse spook at everything? ▼
You don't make it stop. You manage it. A horse's fear response is survival. Start by desensitizing in a safe area. Introduce scary objects at a distance. Reward curiosity or relaxation. Never punish a spook.
How do I bond with my horse? ▼
Go to the pasture without a halter. Sit down nearby. Let the horse choose to approach you. Groom without purpose. Build thousands of small, pressure-free moments where your presence is simply comfortable.
My horse won't stand still at the mounting block. ▼
First, check saddle fit and back soreness. Practice standing without mounting. Reward even a second of stillness. Teach the horse that standing still leads to comfort, not more work.
How do I know if my horse trusts me? ▼
Voluntary approach. A lowered head when you are near. Slow, deep breathing. Leaning into your touch. These are the clearest signs of genuine trust.
My horse bites when I do up the girth. ▼
This is almost always a pain response. Have a vet check for ulcers or back soreness. Use a soft girth. Tighten slowly, one hole per minute, giving a treat after each hole.

The most honest communication with a horse happens in silence. Not when you are asking. In between.

Presence is not a concept. It is the quality of being fully here. Horses live there permanently.
