About Me

Not a Trainer.
A Student of Horses.

Eleven years ago, a black stallion changed everything. Not through a lesson I planned, but through a moment of complete stillness — a moment where I finally stopped trying to be heard and started trying to listen. That moment became the foundation of everything I do today.

I am Praveen Kumar, and I travel across India to work with horses and the people who love them. I do not use fear, force, or dominance. I use understanding. Every method I apply is built on one principle: the horse is never the problem. The horse is always the honest answer to a question the human has not yet learned to ask.

My work covers trust-based natural horsemanship, behavioural rehabilitation for horses labelled difficult or dangerous, and starting young horses in a way that builds genuine willingness. I work with children discovering horses for the first time, adults rebuilding their confidence, experienced riders whose horses have stopped cooperating, and horse owners who simply want to understand why their animal behaves the way it does.

"I do not come to your stable with a programme. I come with patience, with presence, and with eleven years of learning that the horse is always right — and the only question worth asking is whether we are honest enough to hear what it is telling us."

Common Questions

Real Answers for Real Problems

The questions I hear most from horse owners across India.

My horse is aggressive and bites. Is it dangerous and can it be fixed?

A horse that bites is not dangerous — it is communicating. Biting is almost always a last resort, used only when every softer signal has been ignored or punished. Before a horse bites, it has already pinned its ears, tensed its body, and given a dozen other warnings that were either not seen or not respected. The fix is not punishment. It is learning to read what the horse was saying before it reached the point of biting — and then responding to those earlier signals honestly. In eleven years, I have never met an aggressive horse. I have met many horses in pain or horses whose communication was never listened to.

My horse refuses to load into a trailer no matter what I try. What can I do?

Trailer refusal is one of the most common problems I am called to address, and it is almost entirely a fear problem rather than a stubbornness problem. The horse is not refusing to be difficult. It is refusing because walking into a small, dark, enclosed metal box goes against every survival instinct it has. Forcing it in with ropes or whips confirms to the horse that the trailer is exactly as dangerous as it feared. The solution is a slow, systematic process of building positive association with the trailer over several sessions. With the right approach, most horses will load willingly within a few days to a week.

My horse is extremely spooky and bolts at small things. How do I make it calmer?

A spooky horse is almost always a horse that does not fully trust the human beside it. When a horse truly trusts you, it looks to you when something frightens it — using your calmness to calibrate its own response. A horse that bolts has decided, for good reason, that you are not a reliable source of safety. The work is in becoming steadier yourself — quieting your own nervous system, breathing deliberately, and learning to read the early warning signs of rising anxiety so you can help the horse settle before it explodes.

Is natural horsemanship suitable for horses in India, where conditions are different?

Completely. The principles of trust-based horsemanship are not cultural — they are biological. A horse's nervous system, fear response, and communication signals are identical whether the horse lives in Rajasthan, Maharashtra, or Uttarakhand. In India, many horses have had extensive experience with forceful handling, which means the first work is often simply demonstrating that not every human interaction will be painful or frightening. Horses here respond to patient, honest handling exactly as they do anywhere else in the world. Often faster, because the contrast with what they have known is so significant.

My child wants to learn riding. How do I know if a trainer is right for them?

Watch how the trainer treats the horse, not just how they treat your child. A trainer who is rough, impatient, or dismissive with the horse will eventually be the same with a child who struggles. The right trainer will spend time helping your child understand the horse before they ever get into the saddle — because a child who understands how a horse thinks will be safer, calmer, and more confident than one who has simply been told where to put their heels.

How long does it take to fix a problem behaviour in a horse?

It depends on how long the problem has been there, and how willing the human is to change alongside the horse. A horse owner who genuinely commits to changing how they handle their horse will see results far faster than one who wants the horse to change while they stay the same. What I can tell you is that real, lasting change is always possible. I have not yet met a horse that was beyond reach — only horses that had not yet found a human patient enough to reach them.

Do I need any experience with horses before working with you?

None at all. In some ways, less experience is easier to work with than years of habits that do not serve the horse. Whether you have never touched a horse or have been riding for twenty years, we begin from the same place: understanding how the horse experiences the world, and building a relationship from that understanding outward. The only requirement is genuine curiosity and the willingness to be honest — with the horse and with yourself.

My horse was fine for years and has suddenly started misbehaving. Why?

A sudden change in behaviour is almost always either a physical problem or a change in the horse's environment or handling. Before anything else, rule out pain — a saddle that no longer fits, dental issues, back soreness, hoof problems. Horses do not misbehave out of nowhere. The most important first step is a thorough physical check by a good vet, followed by an honest assessment of whether anything has changed in how the horse is being handled, worked, or stabled.
